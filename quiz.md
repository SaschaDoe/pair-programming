## Now let's get specific.

*The next questions are about pair programming — two people, one screen, thinking out loud together. Let that image sit for a moment before you answer.*

## Reflection

This isn't a test with right or wrong answers.

But look at your pattern. If you gravitated toward **c** and **d** answers — that doesn't mean something is wrong with you. It means you're human, working in a profession that constantly asks you to perform your thinking *in public*.

Programming is one of the only jobs where your **thought process becomes an artifact** that others inspect, critique, and rewrite. That takes a special kind of courage — the kind nobody talks about in stand-ups.

Pair programming doesn't just ask you to share a screen. It asks you to **share your doubt, your confusion, your half-baked ideas, and your mistakes — in real time, with no edit button.**

That's not a technical challenge. That's a deeply human one.

The real question isn't *"how good of a programmer are you?"*

It's: **"Do you feel safe enough to be bad at something in front of someone else?"**

Because that's where all the learning happens.
